EU AI Act readiness questions

These answers provide an operational starting point. The exact obligations depend on the organization’s role, systems, context and applicable provisions.

The EU AI Act is Regulation (EU) 2024/1689. It creates harmonised rules for artificial intelligence and assigns obligations according to the legal role of the organization and the risk category of each AI system.

Not every company has the same obligations. Applicability depends on whether the organization develops, provides, deploys, imports, distributes or otherwise uses AI systems covered by the Regulation.

A practical first step is to create an AI system inventory, identify the organization’s legal role for every system, determine the applicable risk category and record the evidence supporting each conclusion.

It is a controlled register of the AI systems an organization uses, develops or supplies, including purpose, provider, owner, data, users, integrations, risk classification, controls and applicable obligations.

High-risk systems are systems falling within the categories defined by the Regulation, including certain safety components and specific use cases. They are subject to enhanced governance, documentation, monitoring and oversight duties.

Article 50 contains transparency obligations for certain systems, including specific situations involving interaction with natural persons, synthetic content, deepfakes, emotion recognition and biometric categorisation.

AI literacy refers to the knowledge, skills and understanding needed by people dealing with AI systems to use them responsibly while considering context, risks and affected persons.

A company may benefit from maintaining proportionate evidence showing how it assessed whether it develops, supplies or uses AI systems. This makes the conclusion reviewable and avoids an unsupported declaration.

Structured documents can be generated from verified company and system parameters, but they should be reviewed for legal role, factual accuracy, responsibilities, system-specific risks and supporting evidence before operational use.
